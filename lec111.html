IB Chemistry 1.1.1: Classification of Matter

Venkata Pendyala

1. Overview

Matter — anything that has mass and occupies space — is classified by composition into elements, compounds, and mixtures. This framework helps predict properties, behavior, and appropriate separation methods.

Modern classification grew from Dalton’s atomic theory and laws such as Lavoisier’s conservation of mass and Proust’s law of definite proportions.

2. Elements

Definition

An element is a pure chemical substance consisting of only one type of atom; it cannot be chemically decomposed into simpler substances.

Each element has a unique atomic number (proton count) and a symbol (often Latin-derived). Elements can appear as monoatomic gases (He), diatomic molecules (\(\ce{O2}\), \(\ce{N2}\)), or polyatomic forms (e.g., \(\ce{P4}\), \(\ce{S8}\)).

Examples: \(\ce{Fe}\) (iron), \(\ce{Au}\) (gold), \(\ce{He}\) (helium).

3. Compounds

Definition

A compound is a pure substance composed of atoms of two or more different elements chemically bonded in a fixed ratio.

Compounds possess properties distinct from their elements. Composition is constant (law of definite proportions). Example: reactive sodium and toxic chlorine combine \(1{:}1\) to form table salt \(\ce{NaCl}\).

Examples: \(\ce{H2O}\), \(\ce{NaCl}\), \(\ce{CO2}\).

4. Mixtures

Definition

A mixture is a physical combination of two or more elements or compounds, with no fixed ratio and no chemical bonding between components.

Components retain their properties and can be separated by physical methods. Mixtures may be homogeneous (uniform; e.g., air, saltwater, brass) or heterogeneous (non‑uniform; e.g., granite, sand + water).

Examples: air, seawater, oil and water.

6. Physical Separation Techniques

6.1 Filtration

Principle: Separates an insoluble solid from a liquid using a porous barrier; liquid passes as the filtrate, solid remains as the residue.

Examples: sand from water; coffee grounds from brew.

Notes: Does not remove dissolved solutes; particle size must exceed pore size.

6.2 Evaporation to Dryness

Principle: Removes solvent by heating, leaving the solute; vapor is not collected.

Examples: obtaining salt from seawater.

Notes: Solvent is lost; heat‑sensitive solutes may decompose.

6.3 Simple Distillation

Principle: Separates liquids (or a liquid from a dissolved solid) by differences in boiling point; the more volatile component is collected as the distillate.

Examples: purifying water from a salt solution.

Notes: Works best when boiling points differ by about \( \ge 50\,^\circ\mathrm{C} \); not suitable for azeotropes/complex mixtures.

7. Practice Problems

  1. Classify each as an element, compound, or mixture. For mixtures, indicate homogeneous or heterogeneous:
    1. Mercury (\(\ce{Hg}\))
    2. Table sugar (\(\ce{C12H22O11}\))
    3. Orange juice with pulp
    4. Air
    5. Brass (Cu–Zn alloy)
  2. Explain why water (\(\ce{H2O}\)) is a compound rather than a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen (reference fixed composition and bonding).
  3. Choose a separation method and justify:
    1. Sand from seawater
    2. Recovering copper(II) sulfate from its aqueous solution
    3. Ethanol from water (bp \(78\,^\circ\mathrm{C}\) vs. \(100\,^\circ\mathrm{C}\))
  4. During filtration, the filtrate is cloudy. Give two likely causes and fixes related to technique or apparatus.
  5. Compare “evaporation to dryness” vs. “crystallization” for recovering dissolved solids in terms of purity and crystal habit.
