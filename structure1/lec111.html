IB Chemistry 1.1.1: Classification of Matter

Venkata Pendyala

1) Overview

Matter—anything that has mass and occupies space—is classified by composition into elements, compounds, and mixtures. This framework predicts properties, behavior, and the appropriate separation methods when substances are combined.

Foundations: Dalton’s atomic theory; Lavoisier’s conservation of mass; Proust’s law of definite proportions; particulate nature of matter.
Key Distinctions: chemical bonding vs. physical mixing; fixed composition vs. variable composition; chemical vs. physical separation.
Notation: Use \(\ce{}\) for chemical formulae; state symbols \((\mathrm{s}), (\mathrm{l}), (\mathrm{g}), (\mathrm{aq})\).

2) Elements

Definition

An element is a pure chemical substance consisting of only one type of atom; it cannot be chemically decomposed into simpler substances.

Essential points

  • Atomic number \(Z\) is unique (proton count); determines the element.
  • Natural forms vary: monoatomic gases (\(\ce{He}\)), diatomic molecules (\(\ce{H2}\), \(\ce{O2}\), \(\ce{N2}\)), and polyatomic allotropes (\(\ce{O3}\), \(\ce{P4}\), \(\ce{S8}\)).
  • Isotopes: same \(Z\), different neutron number (e.g., \(\ce{^{12}C}\), \(\ce{^{13}C}\), \(\ce{^{14}C}\)). Chemical properties similar; physical properties (mass, density) differ.
  • Allotropes: different structural forms of the same element (e.g., carbon as diamond, graphite, graphene).

Examples

  • \(\ce{Fe}\) (iron), \(\ce{Au}\) (gold), \(\ce{Cu}\) (copper)
  • \(\ce{O2}\) (oxygen gas), \(\ce{N2}\) (nitrogen gas)
  • \(\ce{S8}\) (rhombic sulfur), \(\ce{P4}\) (white phosphorus)

3) Compounds

Definition

A compound is a pure substance composed of atoms of two or more different elements chemically bonded in a fixed ratio.

Key ideas

  • Fixed composition: law of definite proportions. \(\ce{H2O}\) is always hydrogen and oxygen in a \(2{:}1\) atomic ratio.
  • New properties: emergent behavior distinct from constituent elements (e.g., \(\ce{Na}\) + \(\ce{Cl2}\) \(\to\) \(\ce{NaCl}\)).
  • Bonding types: ionic (e.g., \(\ce{NaCl}\)), covalent molecular (e.g., \(\ce{CO2}\), \(\ce{C6H12O6}\)), covalent network (e.g., \(\ce{SiO2}\)), metallic (in elemental metals/alloys).
  • Formulas: empirical vs. molecular; formula unit for ionic lattices.

Examples

  • \(\ce{H2O}\), \(\ce{CO2}\), \(\ce{NH3}\)
  • \(\ce{NaCl}\), \(\ce{CaCO3}\)
  • \(\ce{SiO2}\) (quartz: network covalent)

4) Mixtures

Definition

A mixture is a physical combination of two or more elements or compounds, with no fixed ratio and no chemical bonding between components.

Classes of mixtures

  • Homogeneous (solutions): uniform at the particle level; e.g., air (solution of gases), saline \(\ce{NaCl(aq)}\), brass (solid solution of \(\ce{Cu}\) and \(\ce{Zn}\)).
  • Heterogeneous: visibly non-uniform; e.g., granite, oil + water, sand + water.
  • Colloids & suspensions: intermediate particle sizes; Tyndall effect often observed for colloids.

Separation consequences

  • Components retain properties → use physical methods (filtration, distillation, evaporation, crystallization, chromatography, decantation, centrifugation, magnetic separation).
  • Composition is variable: properties (boiling point range, density) vary with proportions.

6) Physical Separation Techniques

6.1 Filtration

Principle: Separates an insoluble solid from a liquid via a porous medium; liquid passes as the filtrate, solid remains as the residue.

When to use

  • Solid is insoluble at the working temperature.
  • Particles are larger than pore size of the filter medium.
  • Goal: remove suspended solids (clarify) or collect the solid.

Procedure (gravity)

  1. Fold filter paper (cone) and seat in funnel; wet with solvent to seal.
  2. Pour mixture along a glass rod to avoid splashing and channeling.
  3. Rinse residue with small portions of solvent to improve recovery.

Notes: Does not remove dissolved solutes; for fine particles or slow flow, use vacuum (Büchner) filtration.

Troubleshooting: Cloudy filtrate → filter pores too large, paper torn, or decanting too quickly; refit paper, use finer grade, or refilter.

6.2 Simple Distillation

Principle: Differences in volatility (boiling point) drive separation. Vapor enriched in the more volatile component condenses and is collected.

Use cases

  • Liquid + nonvolatile solute (e.g., \(\ce{NaCl(aq)}\) → water distillate).
  • Two miscible liquids with sufficiently different boiling points (\(\gtrsim \SI{50}{\celsius}\)).

Limitations

  • Poor resolution when boiling points are close; use fractional distillation.
  • Azeotropes (e.g., ethanol–water at \(\approx \SI{95}{\%}\) \(\ce{C2H5OH}\)) cannot be separated to purity by simple distillation.

Safety/technique: Use boiling chips or stir bars; monitor temperature at the head of the column; never distill to dryness.

6.3 Evaporation to Dryness

Principle: Solvent is vaporized by heating; solute remains. No attempt is made to collect the solvent vapor.

When to use

  • Recovering a nonvolatile solute from solution (e.g., salt from seawater).
  • Pre-concentrating solutions prior to crystallization.

Considerations

  • Solvent is lost; choose distillation if solvent recovery is desired.
  • Heat-sensitive solutes may decompose—prefer gentle heating or reduced pressure.

7) Worked Examples

Example A — Is water a compound?
Water is \(\ce{H2O}\) with a fixed \(2{:}1\) atomic ratio (by moles) and specific bonding geometry. A mixture of \(\ce{H2}\) and \(\ce{O2}\) would have variable proportions and no chemical bonds between them → water is a compound, not a mixture.

Example B — Choose a separation:

  1. Sand from seawater: Filtration (insoluble solid from liquid). Rinse residue with small amounts of water to maximize recovery.
  2. \(\ce{CuSO4(aq)}\) to solid \(\ce{CuSO4}\): Evaporation to dryness (fast, less pure) or better, crystallization with slow cooling for higher purity and larger crystals.
  3. Ethanol (\(\SI{78}{\celsius}\)) from water (\(\SI{100}{\celsius}\)): Simple distillation works in principle; fractional distillation gives sharper separation.

8) Practice Problems (Qualitative & Quantitative)

  1. Classify each as an element, compound, or mixture. For mixtures, indicate homogeneous or heterogeneous:
    1. Mercury (\(\ce{Hg(l)}\))
    2. Table sugar (\(\ce{C12H22O11(s)}\))
    3. Orange juice with pulp
    4. Air
    5. Brass (Cu–Zn)
  2. Explain why \(\ce{H2O}\) is a compound but seawater is a mixture, referencing fixed composition, bonding, and separation method.
  3. Select the best method and justify briefly:
    1. Silica sand from river water
    2. \(\ce{KNO3(aq)}\) to recover solid \(\ce{KNO3}\)
    3. Hexane (\(\mathrm{bp}\approx \SI{69}{\celsius}\)) from toluene (\(\mathrm{bp}\approx \SI{111}{\celsius}\))
  4. A cloudy filtrate is obtained when filtering a clay–water mixture. Give two likely causes and a remedy for each.
  5. Contrast evaporation to dryness and crystallization in terms of expected purity, yield, and crystal habit.
  6. In a simple distillation of \(\ce{NaCl(aq)}\), the thermometer reads \(\SI{99}{\celsius}\)–\(\SI{101}{\celsius}\). Explain the reading and identify the expected composition of the distillate and residue.
Tip: You can write temperatures as \(100\,^\circ\mathrm{C}\) or with the macro \(\SI{100}{\celsius}\). Chemical states: \(\ce{NaCl(aq)}\), \(\ce{CO2(g)}\), \(\ce{H2O(l)}\), \(\ce{Fe(s)}\).
