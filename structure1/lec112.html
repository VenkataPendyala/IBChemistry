IB Chemistry 1.1.2: Kinetic Molecular Theory and States of Matter

Venkata Pendyala

1. Understanding

Kinetic Molecular Theory

The kinetic molecular theory (KMT) models the physical properties of matter in different states (solids, liquids, gases) and explains changes of state. Matter is composed of tiny particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) in constant motion; their behavior depends on particle kinetic energy and the strength of intermolecular forces.

KMT accounts for macroscopic properties such as shape, volume, compressibility, diffusion, and density, and it explains how energy transfer affects phase changes at the particle level.

2. Learning Outcomes

2.1 Distinguishing States of Matter

Solids, liquids, and gases differ in particle arrangement, motion, and the strength of intermolecular forces.

Property Solid (s) Liquid (l) Gas (g)
Shape Fixed Takes container shape Fills container
Volume Fixed Fixed Variable
Particle arrangement Closely packed, ordered Close, random arrangement Far apart, random
Particle motion Vibrates in place Slides past neighbors Moves freely at high speed
Forces between particles Strong Moderate Weak

2.2 Using State Symbols in Chemical Equations

State symbols follow the formula:

  • (s) — solid
  • (l) — liquid
  • (g) — gas
  • (aq) — aqueous (dissolved in water)

Example: dissolution of sodium chloride

\(\ce{NaCl_{(s)} -> Na+_{(aq)} + Cl-_{(aq)}}\)

3. Changes of State

Phase changes occur when energy (usually heat) is added or removed. During a change of state:

  • Temperature remains constant at the phase transition.
  • Energy goes into changing potential energy (overcoming/forming intermolecular forces), not kinetic energy.

3.1 Names of Changes of State

Change From → To Description
Melting Solid → Liquid Particles gain energy and overcome some intermolecular forces.
Freezing Liquid → Solid Particles lose energy and form a fixed, ordered structure.
Vaporization (Boiling) Liquid → Gas Rapid vapor formation throughout the liquid at the boiling point.
Evaporation Liquid → Gas Slow surface process below the boiling point.
Condensation Gas → Liquid Particles lose energy and form a closer, liquid structure.
Sublimation Solid → Gas Direct transition without becoming liquid.
Deposition Gas → Solid Direct transition without becoming liquid.

4. Additional Notes

  • KMT explains compressibility (gases compress most), diffusion rates (fastest in gases), and relative densities (gases \(\ll\) liquids \(\lt\) solids).
  • During phase changes, potential energy changes while average kinetic energy—and thus temperature—stays constant.
  • Physical vs. chemical changes: physical changes (e.g., melting ice) do not alter chemical identity; chemical changes produce new substances with different compositions and properties.

5. Practice Problems

  1. For each, identify the state of matter and explain using particle arrangement and motion:
    1. Ice at \(\SI{0}{\celsius}\)
    2. Steam at \(\SI{100}{\celsius}\)
    3. Molten sodium
  2. Write balanced equations with correct state symbols:
    1. Combustion of methane:
      \(\ce{CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O}\)
    2. Dissolution of potassium hydroxide in water:
      \(\ce{KOH -> K+ + OH-}\)
  3. Explain why temperature remains constant during melting even as heat is applied.
  4. A sample of dry ice \(\ce{(CO2_{(s)})}\) is left on a bench at room temperature. State the change of state and describe it via KMT.
  5. Compare evaporation and boiling in terms of particle energy, location within the liquid, and temperature.
Notation tips: use \(\ce{}\) for chemical species and \(\SI{}{}\) for units, e.g., \(\SI{100}{\celsius}\), \(\SI{1}{atm}\), \(\ce{H2O(l)}\), \(\ce{CO2(g)}\).
