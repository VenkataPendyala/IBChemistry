IB Chemistry 1.2.1 — Subatomic Particles & Nuclear Symbols

Venkata Pendyala

1) Understanding

Atoms, Nucleus, and Electrons

Atoms contain a positively charged, dense nucleus composed of protons and neutrons (together: nucleons). Electrons, which are negatively charged and much less massive, occupy the space outside the nucleus.

Historical perspective

Experiments by J. J. Thomson (cathode rays) revealed the electron and its negative charge. Rutherford’s gold-foil experiment (Geiger–Marsden) showed most alpha particles pass through thin metal foil while a few deflect strongly — evidence for a small, dense, positively charged nucleus. Chadwick later identified the neutron, explaining isotopes as atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Key inference: Almost all atomic mass resides in the nucleus; electrons determine most chemical behavior.

2) Relative Masses and Charges

For qualitative work, we use relative masses/charges. (Actual values are in the IB data booklet; the electron’s mass is often treated as negligible.)

Particle Symbol Relative Charge Relative Mass (≈ proton = 1) Location
Proton p or \(\ce{^1_1H^+}\) \(+1\) \(\approx 1\) Nucleus
Neutron n or \(\ce{^1_0n}\) \(0\) \(\approx 1\) Nucleus
Electron e\(^-\) \(-1\) \(\approx 1/1836\) (often negligible) Outside nucleus

3) Nuclear Symbol & Counting Particles

The nuclear symbol encodes composition and (optionally) ionic charge:

Atoms vs. Ions

  • Neutral atom: \(e = p = Z\).
  • Cation \(X^{q+}\): electrons are lost: \(e = Z - q\).
  • Anion \(X^{q-}\): electrons are gained: \(e = Z + q\).

Isotopes

Same element (same \(Z\)) with different neutron number \(n\). Example: \(\ce{^{12}C}\), \(\ce{^{13}C}\), \(\ce{^{14}C}\) all have \(p=6\) but \(n=6,7,8\) respectively.

4) Worked Examples

Example A — Count particles in an ion.

\(\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe^{3+}}\): \(p=26\); \(n=56-26=30\); \(e=26-3=23\).

Example B — Build the nuclear symbol.

A bromine anion has 36 electrons and 44 neutrons. What is its symbol? Since Br has \(Z=35\), \(p=35\). If \(e=36\), the charge is \(1-\). Neutrons \(n=44\) gives \(A=p+n=79\). Symbol: \(\ce{^{79}_{35}Br^-}\).

5) Practice Problems (Challenging)

  1. For each species, determine \(p\), \(n\), and \(e\).
    1. \(\ce{^{64}_{29}Cu}\)
    2. \(\ce{^{40}_{20}Ca^{2+}}\)
    3. \(\ce{^{34}_{16}S^{2-}}\)
    4. \(\ce{^{127}_{53}I^-}\)
  2. A neutral atom \(X\) has 35 protons and 44 neutrons. Another isotope \(Y\) of the same element has a \(2-\) charge and 46 neutrons.
    1. Write the nuclear symbol for \(X\).
    2. Write the nuclear symbol for \(Y\).
    3. How many electrons does \(Y\) have?
  3. An ion has 28 electrons and is written as \(\ce{^{62}_{?}Ni^{2+}}\). Determine \(Z\), \(A\), and the neutron number \(n\). (Nickel’s \(Z\) is in the periodic table.)
  4. An element \(E\) forms \(\ce{E^{3+}}\) with 22 electrons and has 26 neutrons. Determine:
    1. \(Z\) and the identity of \(E\)
    2. Mass number \(A\)
    3. Nuclear symbol
  5. Two isotopes of titanium occur: \(\ce{^{47}Ti}\) (7.44%) and \(\ce{^{49}Ti}\) (5.41%). The remaining is \(\ce{^{48}Ti}\).
    1. Estimate the average mass (no need for exact atomic-mass values; use mass numbers).
    2. Which isotope must be the most abundant? Explain.
  6. A species is reported as \(\ce{^{A}_{Z}Cl^{q-}}\) with \(e=18\) and \(n=18\).
    1. Find \(Z\), \(q\), and \(A\).
    2. Write the full symbol.
  7. (Synthesis) A sample contains a mix of \(\ce{^{24}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{25}Mg}\), and \(\ce{^{26}Mg}\). The average atomic mass is measured as \(\approx \SI{24.3}{u}\). Propose a plausible set of isotope abundances (percentages) that yield this average, and justify briefly.

6) Notes & Exam Tips

  • The mass number \(A\) counts nucleons \(A=p+n\); the atomic number \(Z\) counts protons.
  • Electrons change only with charge: \(e = Z - (\text{charge as positive integer for cations})\) or \(e = Z + (\text{charge for anions})\).
  • Electron mass is negligible at IB precision; nuclear mass dominates.
  • Always check that your nuclear symbol’s \(Z\) matches the element symbol you wrote.
