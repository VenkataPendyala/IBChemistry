IB Chemistry 1.2.1 — Subatomic Particles & Nuclear Symbols

Venkata Pendyala

1) Understanding

Atoms, Nucleus, and Electrons

Atoms contain a positively charged, dense nucleus composed of protons and neutrons (nucleons). Electrons, which are negatively charged and much less massive, occupy the space outside the nucleus.

2) Relative Masses and Charges

For qualitative work, we use relative masses/charges. The electron’s mass is often treated as negligible in calculations.

ParticleSymbolRelative ChargeRelative MassLocation
Protonp+1≈1Nucleus
Neutronn0≈1Nucleus
Electrone⁻−1≈1/1836Outside nucleus

3) Nuclear Symbol & Counting Particles

Atoms vs. Ions

  • Neutral atom: \(e = p = Z\).
  • Cation \(X^{q+}\): electrons lost → \(e = Z - q\).
  • Anion \(X^{q-}\): electrons gained → \(e = Z + q\).

Isotopes

Same element (same \(Z\)) with different neutron number \(n\). Example: \(\ce{^{12}C}\), \(\ce{^{13}C}\), \(\ce{^{14}C}\) all have \(p=6\) but \(n=6,7,8\) respectively.

Exam tip: Always check that the symbol’s \(Z\) matches the element you wrote.

4) Worked Examples

Example A — Count particles in an ion. \(\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe^{3+}}\): \(p=26\); \(n=56-26=30\); \(e=26-3=23\).

Example B — Build the nuclear symbol. A bromine anion has 36 electrons and 44 neutrons. Br has \(Z=35\) → \(p=35\). If \(e=36\), charge \(=1-\). Neutrons \(n=44\) → \(A=79\). Symbol: \(\ce{^{79}_{35}Br^-}\).

5) Practice Problems (Challenging)

  1. For each species, determine \(p\), \(n\), and \(e\).
    1. \(\ce{^{64}_{29}Cu}\)
    2. \(\ce{^{40}_{20}Ca^{2+}}\)
    3. \(\ce{^{34}_{16}S^{2-}}\)
    4. \(\ce{^{127}_{53}I^-}\)
  2. A neutral atom \(X\) has 35 protons and 44 neutrons. Another isotope \(Y\) of the same element has a \(2-\) charge and 46 neutrons.
    1. Write the nuclear symbol for \(X\).
    2. Write the nuclear symbol for \(Y\).
    3. How many electrons does \(Y\) have?
  3. An ion has 28 electrons and is written as \(\ce{^{62}_{?}Ni^{2+}}\). Determine \(Z\), \(A\), and the neutron number \(n\).
  4. An element \(E\) forms \(\ce{E^{3+}}\) with 22 electrons and has 26 neutrons. Determine:
    1. \(Z\) and the identity of \(E\)
    2. Mass number \(A\)
    3. Nuclear symbol
  5. Two isotopes of titanium occur: \(\ce{^{47}Ti}\) (7.44%) and \(\ce{^{49}Ti}\) (5.41%). The remaining is \(\ce{^{48}Ti}\).
    1. Estimate the average mass using mass numbers.
    2. Which isotope must be the most abundant? Explain.
  6. A species is reported as \(\ce{^{A}_{Z}Cl^{q-}}\) with \(e=18\) and \(n=18\).
    1. Find \(Z\), \(q\), and \(A\).
    2. Write the full symbol.

6) Notes & Exam Tips

  • \(A = p + n\); \(Z = p\); \(e\) depends on ionic charge.
  • Cations lose \(e^-\); anions gain \(e^-\).
  • Electron mass is negligible at IB precision; nuclear mass dominates.
