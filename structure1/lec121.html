IB Chemistry 1.2.1 — Subatomic Particles & Nuclear Symbols

Venkata Pendyala

1) Understanding

Atoms, Nucleus, and Electrons

Atoms contain a positively charged, dense nucleus composed of protons and neutrons (nucleons). Electrons, which are negatively charged and much less massive, occupy the space outside the nucleus.

2) Relative Masses and Charges

For qualitative work, we use relative masses/charges. The electron’s mass is often treated as negligible in calculations.

4) Worked Examples

Example A: For \(\ce{^56_26Fe^{3+}}\): p=26, n=30, e=23.

Example B: Bromine anion with 36 e⁻ and 44 n. Br: Z=35, so A=79, charge = -1 → \(\ce{^{79}_{35}Br^-}\).

5) Practice Problems

  1. Find p, n, e for:

    1. \(\ce{^64_29Cu}\)
    2. \(\ce{^40_20Ca^{2+}}\)
    3. \(\ce{^34_16S^{2-}}\)
    4. \(\ce{^127_{53}I^-}\)

  2. Two isotopes X and Y of same element: X has p=35, n=44; Y has charge 2− and n=46. Identify symbols and electrons.

  3. A Ni²⁺ ion has 28 e⁻ and A=62. Find Z and n.

  4. Element E with 22 e⁻ and n=26, forms E³⁺. Find symbol and Z, A.

  5. Titanium isotopes (47, 48, 49) average ≈24.3 u. Suggest plausible abundances.

  6. Species \(\ce{^A_ZCl^{q-}}\) has e=18 and n=18. Find Z, A, q, and symbol.

6) Notes & Exam Tips

  • A = p + n; Z = p; e depends on ionic charge.
  • For ions: cations lose e, anions gain e.
  • Electron mass is negligible in IB context.
