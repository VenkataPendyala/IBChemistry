IB Chemistry 1.2.2 — Isotopes & Mass Spectra

Venkata Pendyala

1) Understanding

What are isotopes?

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same atomic number \(Z\), hence same number of protons) that differ in their number of neutrons. Therefore they share almost identical chemical behavior (same electron configuration when neutral/ionized similarly) but can have different physical properties (mass-dependent). Examples: \(\ce{^{24}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{25}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{26}Mg}\); \(\ce{^{54}Fe}\), \(\ce{^{56}Fe}\), \(\ce{^{57}Fe}\), \(\ce{^{58}Fe}\).

2) Why relative atomic masses are non-integers

The value on the periodic table (the relative atomic mass, \(A_\mathrm{r}\)) is a weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes:

\[ A_\mathrm{r}(E) \;=\; \sum_i \left(\frac{\text{abundance}_i}{100}\right) \times (\text{isotopic mass})_i \]

Because isotopes occur in different proportions, the average is rarely an integer. For magnesium, the natural mixture of \(\ce{^{24}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{25}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{26}Mg}\) yields \(A_\mathrm{r} \approx 24.3\).

3) Quick primer: mass spectrometry for isotopes

In a mass spectrometer, atoms/molecules are ionized and separated by mass-to-charge ratio \(m/z\). A typical isotope spectrum (for an element) shows several peaks at different \(m/z\) values corresponding to the different isotopes. The peak heights or relative intensities are proportional to their relative abundances.

4) Worked examples

Example A — \(A_\mathrm{r}\) from isotope abundances (Mg).

Suppose a magnesium spectrum shows intensities proportional to 79.0 : 10.0 : 11.0 for \(\ce{^{24}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{25}Mg}\), \(\ce{^{26}Mg}\) respectively (these are illustrative, but close to real values).

\[ A_\mathrm{r}(\ce{Mg}) = 0.790\times 24 + 0.100\times 25 + 0.110\times 26 \]

\(= 18.96 + 2.50 + 2.86 = 24.32 \approx 24.3\)

Note: If you are given raw peak heights, first convert them to percentages (divide each by the total and \(\times 100\)).

Example B — Determine unknown abundances given \(A_\mathrm{r}\) (2-isotope case).

Chlorine has two main isotopes: \(\ce{^{35}Cl}\) and \(\ce{^{37}Cl}\). If \(A_\mathrm{r}(\ce{Cl})=35.45\) and we assume only those two isotopes with mass numbers 35 and 37, find their abundances.

Let \(x\) = fraction of \(\ce{^{35}Cl}\), so \(1-x\) = fraction of \(\ce{^{37}Cl}\). \[ 35.45 = 35x + 37(1-x) = 37 - 2x \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2x = 37 - 35.45 = 1.55 \Rightarrow x = 0.775 \] Therefore \(\ce{^{35}Cl}\) ≈ \(77.5\%\), \(\ce{^{37}Cl}\) ≈ \(22.5\%\).

Example C — Three isotopes from relative peak heights (algebra set-up).

A sample of element \(E\) shows three isotopes with masses 24, 25, and 26. The relative peak heights are \(h_{24}:h_{25}:h_{26} = 79:10:11\). Convert to fractions: \[ f_{24}=\frac{79}{100},\quad f_{25}=\frac{10}{100},\quad f_{26}=\frac{11}{100}. \] Then \[ A_\mathrm{r}(E)=24 f_{24}+25 f_{25}+26 f_{26}. \] Substitute and evaluate to find the average mass (as in Example A).

5) Differences in physical properties of isotopes

Because isotopes differ in mass, they can have small but measurable differences in physical properties. Chemical properties are essentially identical (same \(Z\), same valence electron configuration).

Property Trend across isotopes Notes/Consequences
Mass & density Heavier isotope → slightly higher density Used in isotope separation; affects diffusion rates (Graham’s law).
Diffusion rate (gases) Lighter isotope → faster diffusion Basis for enrichment methods; measurable in effusion experiments.
Melting/boiling point Small shifts Isotope effects are usually minor but real (vibrational zero-point energy differences).
Bond vibrational frequency Heavier isotopes vibrate at lower frequency IR spectra shift slightly (e.g., \(\ce{C-H}\) vs. \(\ce{C-D}\)).

6) How to read an isotope mass spectrum (element)

  1. Identify peaks: Each distinct \(m/z\) near the integer mass corresponds to a different isotope’s singly charged ion (usually \(z=+1\) → \(m/z \approx \text{mass}\)).
  2. Measure relative intensities: Read peak heights/areas and convert to fractions or percentages.
  3. Compute \(A_\mathrm{r}\): Use the weighted-average formula with isotopic masses.
  4. Check reasonableness: The final \(A_\mathrm{r}\) should lie between the lightest and heaviest isotope masses.

7) Practice Problems (Challenging)

  1. An element \(X\) has two isotopes of masses 64 and 66. Its mass spectrum shows peaks with relative heights 3:2 (for 64:66).
    1. Calculate the percent abundance of each isotope.
    2. Compute \(A_\mathrm{r}(X)\).
  2. The following simplified magnesium spectrum (masses 24, 25, 26) has relative intensities 78.6 : 10.1 : 11.3.
    1. Convert these intensities to fractional abundances.
    2. Calculate \(A_\mathrm{r}(\ce{Mg})\) and compare to the periodic-table value \(\approx 24.3\).
  3. Bromine has two main isotopes (\(\ce{^{79}Br}\) and \(\ce{^{81}Br}\)). A mass spectrum of molecular bromine, \(\ce{Br2}\), shows three major peaks at \(m/z \approx 158, 160, 162\).
    1. Explain the origin of these three peaks (combinatorics of isotope pairs).
    2. If the central peak at 160 is the most intense, what does that imply about the relative abundances of \(\ce{^{79}Br}\) and \(\ce{^{81}Br}\)?
  4. Element \(E\) has three isotopes with masses 10.01, 11.01, and 12.01 u. The mass spectrum gives relative intensities in the ratio 19 : 80 : 1 (respectively).
    1. Determine the percent abundance of each isotope.
    2. Calculate \(A_\mathrm{r}(E)\).
  5. A diatomic gas shows a parent peak at \(m/z=70\) and a second peak at \(m/z=72\). The 70 peak is roughly nine times higher than the 72 peak. Assuming both atoms are the same element with two isotopes differing by 2 u, deduce the relative abundance of the heavier isotope. (Hint: consider all pairings in the molecule.)
  6. Qualitatively predict how substitution of \(\ce{^2H}\) (deuterium) for \(\ce{^1H}\) in \(\ce{H2O}\) would affect:
    1. Diffusion rate of the vapor
    2. Vibrational frequencies in IR spectra
    3. Boiling point (very small effect, but in which direction?)
