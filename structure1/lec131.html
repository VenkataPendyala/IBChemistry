IB Chemistry 1.2.3 — Emission Spectra & The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Venkata Pendyala

1) Understanding

What produces an emission spectrum?

When atoms absorb energy, their electrons can be promoted to excited states. As these electrons return to lower energy levels, the atom emits photons. Each transition releases a photon with energy \[ E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}, \] so only specific wavelengths (or frequencies) appear — a line spectrum. In contrast, a continuous spectrum shows every wavelength in a region without gaps.

2) The electromagnetic spectrum: colour, wavelength, frequency, energy

Electromagnetic radiation spans a vast range of wavelengths \((\lambda)\) and frequencies \((\nu)\). These are related by \(c=\lambda\nu\). The photon energy is \(E=h\nu\), so:

  • Shorter \(\lambda\) → higher \(\nu\) → higher \(E\) (e.g., UV, X-rays).
  • Longer \(\lambda\) → lower \(\nu\) → lower \(E\) (e.g., IR, microwaves, radio).
  • Visible light spans roughly \(\lambda \approx \SI{400}{nm}\) (violet, high energy) to \(\approx \SI{700}{nm}\) (red, lower energy).

3) Continuous vs. line spectra

Continuous spectrum

Produced by dense, hot sources (e.g., incandescent solids/liquids, or dense gases). All wavelengths in a range are present — no discrete gaps. A continuous spectrum can later show dark lines (absorption) if cooler gas is in the way.

Line spectra

Produced by excited, low-pressure gases (discrete atomic transitions). Each element has a fingerprint of lines at characteristic wavelengths; classic example: the Balmer series of hydrogen in the visible.

IB focus: You should be able to qualitatively describe the relation between colour, \(\lambda\), \(\nu\), and \(E\); and distinguish continuous vs. line spectra from a description or diagram.

4) Atomic lines, levels, and colours

In atoms, electrons occupy quantized energy levels. A downward transition from level \(n_i\) to \(n_f\) emits a photon of energy \(\Delta E = E_{n_i}-E_{n_f}\). For hydrogen, the Balmer series (to \(n_f=2\)) lies in the visible (H\(\alpha\) red \(\approx\SI{656}{nm}\), H\(\beta\) blue-green \(\approx\SI{486}{nm}\), etc.). Larger \(\Delta E\) → shorter \(\lambda\) → higher \(E\).

5) Worked examples

Example A — From wavelength to energy and colour.

A bright emission line is observed at \(\lambda = \SI{486}{nm}\). \[ \nu=\frac{c}{\lambda}=\frac{3.00\times10^{8}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}}{4.86\times10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m}} \approx 6.17\times10^{14}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}} \] \[ E=h\nu=(6.626\times10^{-34}\ \mathrm{J\,s})(6.17\times10^{14}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}})\approx 4.09\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J} \] This is the H\(\beta\) line (blue-green) in the Balmer series of hydrogen.

Example B — Ranking by energy.

Rank \(\SI{410}{nm}\) (violet), \(\SI{589}{nm}\) (yellow), \(\SI{656}{nm}\) (red) by photon energy. Since \(E\propto 1/\lambda\): \(\SI{410}{nm} > \SI{589}{nm} > \SI{656}{nm}\).

Example C — Identify spectrum type from a scenario.

A hot filament lamp viewed directly: continuous spectrum. The same lamp viewed through a cool sodium vapour: absorption spectrum (dark lines near \(\SI{589}{nm}\)). A discharge tube of low-pressure hydrogen: emission line spectrum (distinct coloured lines).

6) Practice Problems (Challenging)

  1. Light of \(\lambda=\SI{410}{nm}\) (violet) is emitted in an atomic transition.
    1. Calculate \(\nu\) and \(E\) of one photon.
    2. Compare its energy to a \(\SI{656}{nm}\) (red) photon: which is larger and by what factor?
  2. A sodium lamp emits a strong yellow line near \(\SI{589}{nm}\).
    1. Explain why a sodium flame test is characteristically yellow.
    2. Would you expect a continuous, absorption, or emission spectrum? Justify.
  3. A star’s spectrum shows a continuous background with dark lines. Explain the origin of the dark lines and what information they provide about the star’s atmosphere.
  4. Order the following by increasing wavelength (justify with \(E=h\nu\) and \(c=\lambda\nu\)): ultraviolet, infrared, red light, X-rays, microwaves.
  5. A gas discharge tube emits two visible lines at \(\SI{486}{nm}\) and \(\SI{656}{nm}\). Which line corresponds to the larger energy level drop? Explain without calculations, then verify with \(E=hc/\lambda\).
  6. A hydrogen-like ion shows its “Balmer-like” series at shorter wavelengths than hydrogen. Suggest a reason in terms of nuclear charge and level spacings.
