IB Chemistry 1.3.2 — Electronic Configuration & Orbital Theory

Venkata Pendyala

1) Understanding: What an electronic configuration is

Definition

An element’s electronic configuration specifies how its electrons are distributed among atomic orbitals (subshells \(s,p,d,f\)) in the lowest-energy arrangement (the ground state), unless otherwise noted. We write configurations as \(\mathrm{1s^22s^22p^63s^23p^6\ldots}\) or using noble-gas core notation, e.g. \(\mathrm{[Ne]\,3s^23p^4}\).

Orbitals are grouped into subshells \(s\) (1 orbital), \(p\) (3), \(d\) (5), \(f\) (7); each orbital holds at most 2 electrons (opposite spins).

2) The three rules that determine ground-state configurations

Aufbau Principle (Build-up)

Electrons occupy the lowest-energy orbitals available first, following the general energy order: \(\mathrm{1s}\) → \(\mathrm{2s}\) → \(\mathrm{2p}\) → \(\mathrm{3s}\) → \(\mathrm{3p}\) → \(\mathrm{4s}\) → \(\mathrm{3d}\) → \(\mathrm{4p}\) → \(\mathrm{5s}\) → \(\mathrm{4d}\) → \(\mathrm{5p}\) → \(\mathrm{6s}\) → \(\mathrm{4f}\) → \(\mathrm{5d}\) → \(\mathrm{6p}\) → \(\mathrm{7s}\) → \(\mathrm{5f}\) → \(\mathrm{6d}\) → \(\mathrm{7p}\).

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. **Practically:** an orbital holds at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spins (often drawn as ↑↓).

Hund’s Rule (Maximum Multiplicity)

Within a subshell (e.g., \(2p\) has 3 orbitals), electrons occupy empty orbitals singly with parallel spins before pairing. This minimizes electron–electron repulsion and lowers energy.

4) Writing configurations: the bulletproof algorithm

  1. Count electrons: For a neutral atom, e⁻ = atomic number \(Z\). For ions, adjust by charge (add for anions, subtract for cations).
  2. Fill in Aufbau order (diagram above), respecting Pauli and Hund.
  3. Use noble-gas cores when helpful (e.g., \(\mathrm{[Ar]}\), \(\mathrm{[Kr]}\), \(\mathrm{[Xe]}\)).
  4. EXCEPTIONS: Watch the well-known \(d\)-subshell stabilizations (below); for cations of transition metals, remove \(ns\) electrons before \((n-1)d\).
Ionization rule for transition metals: Although \(4s\) fills before \(3d\), upon ionization the \(4s\) electrons are removed first (they are higher in energy in the presence of \(3d\) electrons).

6) The classic exceptions (and why)

Some elements gain stability from half-filled (\(d^5\)) and filled (\(d^{10}\)) subshells due to exchange energy and symmetry. The most-tested:

ElementExpected (naïve) AufbauActual ground stateReason
\(\ce{Cr}\) (24)\(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^4\,4s^2}\)\(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^5\,4s^1}\)Half-filled \(3d\) stabilization.
\(\ce{Cu}\) (29)\(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^9\,4s^2}\)\(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^1}\)Filled \(3d\) stabilization.
\(\ce{Mo}\) (42)\(\mathrm{[Kr]\,4d^4\,5s^2}\)\(\mathrm{[Kr]\,4d^5\,5s^1}\)Half-filled \(4d\) stabilization.
\(\ce{Ag}\) (47)\(\mathrm{[Kr]\,4d^9\,5s^2}\)\(\mathrm{[Kr]\,4d^{10}\,5s^1}\)Filled \(4d\) stabilization.
\(\ce{Au}\) (79)\(\mathrm{[Xe]\,4f^{14}\,5d^9\,6s^2}\)\(\mathrm{[Xe]\,4f^{14}\,5d^{10}\,6s^1}\)Filled \(5d\) stabilization (relativistic effects contribute).

Note: Many heavier atoms may have subtle deviations; the above are the canonical IB-level cases.

7) Noble-gas notation & period mapping (n, n−1, n−2)

  • Main group: Valence for Period \(n\) = \(ns\) and \(np\).
  • Transition series: Period \(n\) has \((n-1)d\) subshells (e.g., Period 4 → \(3d\)).
  • Lanthanides/actinides: \(f\)-block is \((n-2)f\) (e.g., Period 6 lanthanides → \(4f\)).

8) Paramagnetism vs. diamagnetism

  • Paramagnetic: one or more unpaired electrons → attracted to magnetic fields.
  • Diamagnetic: all electrons paired → weakly repelled by magnetic fields.

9) Ions: remove/add in the right place (especially d-block!)

\(\ce{Fe}\) (26): neutral \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^6\,4s^2}\).

  • \(\ce{Fe^{2+}}\): remove \(4s\) first → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^6}\).
  • \(\ce{Fe^{3+}}\): remove one more from \(3d\) → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^5}\) (half-filled \(d\)).

\(\ce{Cu}\) (29): neutral \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^1}\).

  • \(\ce{Cu^+}\): remove \(4s\) → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}}\) (diamagnetic).
  • \(\ce{Cu^{2+}}\): remove \(4s\) and one \(3d\) → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^9}\) (paramagnetic).

10) Worked examples (step-by-step)

Example A — Sulfur \(\ce{S}\) (\(Z=16\)):

  1. Electrons = 16.
  2. Fill: \(\mathrm{1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^6\,3s^2\,3p^4}\).
  3. Noble gas: \(\mathrm{[Ne]\,3s^2\,3p^4}\).
  4. Unpaired e⁻: two in \(3p\) (Hund) → paramagnetic.

Example B — Chromium \(\ce{Cr}\) (\(Z=24\)):

  1. Naïve: \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^4\,4s^2}\).
  2. Actual: \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^5\,4s^1}\) (half-filled \(d\) stabilization).

Example C — \(\ce{Ti^{2+}}\) (\(Z=22\)):

  1. Neutral Ti: \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^2\,4s^2}\).
  2. Remove \(4s\) first (2 e⁻) → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^2}\).

Example D — \(\ce{Br^-}\) (\(Z=35\)):

  1. Neutral Br: \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^2\,4p^5}\).
  2. Gain 1 e⁻ → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^2\,4p^6} = \mathrm{[Kr]}\) (noble-gas configuration).

Example E — \(\ce{Zn^{2+}}\) (\(Z=30\)):

  1. Neutral Zn: \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^2}\).
  2. Remove \(4s^2\) → \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}}\) (fully filled \(d\), diamagnetic).

11) Common mistakes to avoid (read these!)

  • Removing from \(3d\) before \(4s\) in cations: wrong. Always remove \(ns\) before \((n-1)d\) for transition metals.
  • Pairing too early in \(p\)-orbitals: apply Hund’s rule; spread out singles first (↑ ↑ ↑) then pair (↑↓).
  • Forgetting exceptions (Cr, Cu…): half-filled/filled \(d\) subshells stabilize by a small energy gain.
  • Miscounting electrons for ions: cations have fewer electrons than \(Z\); anions have more.
  • Mixing period number with \(d/f\) labels: remember \(3d\) belongs to Period 4, \(4f\) to Period 6.

13) Practice Problems (Challenging & Comprehensive)

  1. Write ground-state configurations (noble-gas form) and state paramagnetic/diamagnetic:
    1. \(\ce{Si}\), \(\ce{P}\), \(\ce{Cl}\)
    2. \(\ce{V}\), \(\ce{Mn}\), \(\ce{Zn}\)
    3. \(\ce{Ag}\), \(\ce{Au}\) (watch exceptions)
  2. For each ion, write the electron configuration and count unpaired electrons:
    1. \(\ce{Fe^{2+}}\), \(\ce{Fe^{3+}}\)
    2. \(\ce{Cu^+}\), \(\ce{Cu^{2+}}\)
    3. \(\ce{Cr^{3+}}\), \(\ce{Mn^{2+}}\)
  3. An unknown metal \(M\) has a \(M^{2+}\) ion that is diamagnetic with configuration \(\mathrm{[Ar]\,3d^{10}}\).
    1. Identify \(M\) and its neutral-atom configuration.
    2. Explain why \(M^{2+}\) is diamagnetic while \(M\) is not necessarily.
  4. Explain, with reference to energy and exchange stabilization, why \(\ce{Cr}\) and \(\ce{Mo}\) adopt \(d^5 s^1\) ground states rather than \(d^4 s^2\).
  5. For a Period 6 element in the lanthanide series, identify which principal level the \(f\)-electrons occupy and write a generic template for its configuration using \(\mathrm{[Xe]}\).
  6. Determine whether the following are paramagnetic or diamagnetic and justify: \(\ce{Ti^{2+}}, \ce{Zn^{2+}}, \ce{Cu^+}, \ce{Mn}, \ce{Ni^{2+}}\).
